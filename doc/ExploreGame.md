# Exploration game design

This game uses existing systems in the market sim.  It builds upon the concepts of Person, Location and Transport, adding new core concepts into
the World being simulated.

In the document below, the terms "Westerner" and "European" are interchangeable.

## World

The World remains a large area of ocean, but now has a single, big island in the middle, many hundreds of miles in radius.  The island is located
in tropical climes, and is largely composed of valleys and hills blanketed in rainforest, with a few mountain peaks.  There is a sizable river network
as well, with a few large rivers being fed by many tributaries.  Dotted around the main island are various smaller islands.  These are largely volcanic,
with white beaches leading to dense vegetation in the interior.

## Setting

This is a time when Europeans have arrived, and are looking to see how this new land can be exploited for their gain.  They are interested primarily in
control of natural resources.  However, various scientific and academic institutes are also interested in the flora, fauna, geology and peoples.  The
European governments and private institutes are sending various expeditions to the island in the hope of progressing their goals.

### Natives

The islands are home to various indigenous tribes.  These tribes are culturally distinct, and geographically concentrated in their own areas.  Tribes are
divided up by village, and each village is ruled over by the local chieftain, who is responsible for everything within the village's borders (which extend
beyond the village itself).

#### Conflict

##### Inter-tribal Conflict

This is constantly happening, and disputes are often over territory, insults (perceived or real) and so on.  Religious differences also play a large part.
Tribes are keen to buy weapons off Westerners, and Westerners can play tribes off against each other, for example treaties, which often include some kind of land
claim on the part of the Westerners in return for preferential trading, mercenary work (eg attacking a village’s enemies).

##### Attitude to Europeans

Chieftains, or headmen as they are often called, are suspicious of Westerners, and have a variety of reasons for this:

- Unfamiliarity, xenophobia, superstition
- Inpingement on local trade agreements
- Rumours and propaganda spread by others
- Fear

Foreign parties need permission to pass through a chieftain's lands.  The chieftains will try and extort as much as possible out of any passing Western party.  They
won't take everything, so that neighbouring chiefs don't blame them when the explorers turned up in their territory with nothing to give.

While theft is a serious crime amongst natives, Europeans are fair game and stealing is rife.
Westerners are often assumed to be spies.  Rulers might send people to arrest them, and often explorers (when solo or in small group) haved to avoid towns/villages.
In this, the locals often help them by warning them not to enter, but the explorer will need to consider carefully which habitations are “safe”.
Even if not assumed to be spies, those who are threatened (eg local traders) might spread rumours/lies.
Rulers rarely believe that explorers are there just to explore - it makes no sense to them - and they will assume it is for trade of some kind

Conflict with Westerners is more common along rivers: rivers allow people to penetrate further into the country, encountering peoples who are not so accustomed to
them.  Parties would often be intercepted by canoes, or attacked from the riverside.

#### Gifts

As chieftains were often engaging in fighting other tribes, weapons are popular gifts.  Tobacco is another.  For example, a Westerner might be arraigned because they
don’t realise they have to pay the duty beforehand, and ends up bribing those who have been sent after them.  They might even be killed for not giving gifts.

Gifts/rewards are often pressed for at every opportunity, in return for a “favour”.  But, sometimes just being firm and having guns is enough to dissuade the
extortion attempts.

Sometimes a ruler will make an agreement with another to send travellers their way, and while they will not enforce this, if travellers wanted to go elsewhere they won’t provide guides.  Westerners might have to pay their way as part of a deal struck between two tribes.  Other leaders on hearing of this will try to extort them
as well.

At a local ruler’s “court”, travellers will often be obliged to make gifts of clothes and other unusual items.  They may have to attend to the medical issues both
of the ruler and the women.  Chiefs may give advice/intelligence (presumably if they’ve received good gifts): for instance they might warn travellers about another
tribe lying in wait for them.

### Trade

Various towns and trading posts have sprung up on the satellite islands, and on the coast of the central island.  There are also a small number of inland
settlements - not far from the coast - which are connected via forest trails and the occasional open scrubland.  These settlements have been founded by earlier
explorers, for the purpose of provisioning and trade with the natives.  Various natively-produced items are traded, such as food, furs, skins, ivory, spices, rubber
dye pigments and minerals such as gold, silver, cobalt and tin.  Imported goods are mostly weapons, textiles and cloth, tobacco and alcohol.  Cowrie shells are a
local currency which are highly sought after by natives.  Brightly-coloured beads - blue, in particular - are also in great demand, as are pieces of amber.

Trade doesn't just happen at settlements, but also when meeting people en-route.  Some settlements have a major export, due to proximity to particular natural
resources, for instance, salt.

Trade at villages incurs fees from the chieftain.  This is generally taxed per loaded animal, and isn't just on foreigners, but any trader.

### Natural world

#### Agriculture

The following crops and foods are grown: yam, plaintain, sweet potato, maize, pumpkins, pawpaw, pineapple, millet, okra, manioc, vanilla, rice, mango, oranges.

#### Flora

Sugar cane, coffee, cocoa, cinchona, swordgrass, breadfruit, ebony tree, ciboa palm, cannabis.

#### Fauna

Porcupines, pythons, hedgehogs, bats, locusts (often cover trees in swarms, turning them black), frogs (very loud at night), hornbills, ostriches (found in more
arid areas), wild boars (which are bad-tempered and dangerous), antelope, monkeys, kingfishers, turtles, snakes, centipedes and scorpions (often found inside),
elephants, leopards (common), lions, gorillas, jackals, crocodiles, ants (bites are painful, last for days), large snails, zebras, hippos, bees (the angry type,
they come after rum and othe sweet things, and can scatter animal packs).

#### Disease

Malaria, yellow fever, dengue fever, tsetse fly (animals), filariasis, ringworm, guinea worm, lassa fever, dysentry, leprosy, elephantiasis.

Quinine can help with some of these.

Disease is much more rift in the wet season.  When dying, people would often just lie down by the side of the path, to die on their own, being to fatigured to
continue, even on an animal.

#### Common illnesses
 
Common conditions experienced by explorers are: malnutrition, temporary blindness caused by fever, dehydration, haemorrhoids, heat exhaustion (which also affects
pack animals), and foot injuries such as abcesses.
